The Biological Dance of Agency

April 24, 2026

There is a curious symbiosis in the heated battles of the animal kingdom. Whether it's predators and prey or two alphas fighting for territory, their movements often fall into a strange, synchronized rhythm. They lunge in bursts of violence, then, in perfect sync, take a breather. It’s as if their instinctual drive is turned up to eleven until exhaustion momentarily subverts it, only for the cycle to repeat seconds later.

As humans, we observe this rhythm in boxing matches where opponents rotate in defensive trances, each waiting for a weakness that may not come for minutes. But unlike other biological entities, humans possess the unique ability to overcome these natural predilections through agency. We can anticipate the "lull" in the action and strike when the opponent is out of rhythm.

This biological dance extends into our modern lives—our careers, our self-discipline, and our habits. We are surrounded by distractions that tempt us back to our "default settings," as David Foster Wallace described them. We fall into lulls of instant gratification and easy escapes, often only working in bursts when external forces implore us to act.

However, the complex human brain allows us to be skeptical of our own inherent urges. We don't have to be slaves to our innate instinctual predilections. We can exercise our agency to push through the inevitable lulls with sheer violence toward our defined goals. We can sacrifice the present for the future because we have the will to outperform the rhythm of the moment. By identifying the opportunity and pushing the attack when others submit to the lull, we can win the battle. And it all begins with preparation.

Curating Meaning in an AI World

April 23, 2026

I've long been fascinated by a simple realization I had in high school: there is so much music in the world that you couldn't possibly listen to it all in one lifetime. That was before the streaming explosion of Netflix and Hulu, and long before the recent AI boom.

Historically, humanity rallied around shared artifacts. Generations passed down the great texts, the works of Shakespeare, the Bible, or the enduring statues of antiquity. Whether by brute force (think the everlasting physical presence of the Pyramids) or logical persuasion (everyone just kept agreeing and couldn't deny the greatness), the human race agreed these ideas should be shared. We huddled around the boombox and packed into movie theaters for collective experiences, naturally moving toward a common, shared narrative.

The Walkman started a quiet shift that modern algorithms have perfected. Today, our individualized devices and algorithmic feeds shape our perception of the world entirely to our own liking. While this brings great joy and allows us to deeply explore niche ideas with like-minded people, it stymies our tendency to align on a shared human existence.

Now, with AI accelerating content creation to infinity and giving everyone a platform, the most vital question isn't how to consume more. The question is exactly what we should choose to consume, and how we find our common ground again.
